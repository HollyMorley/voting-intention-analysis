UK Voting Intention Explorer

How a survey of 1,468 people maps onto party support, and what matters to voters.

The interactive plots in this page allow you to explore the survey results, and insights from a random forest model trained to predict voting intentions. This includes:

  1. Visualising the economic and nationalism values of respondents intending to vote for different parties
  2. Model insights — what demographic features and questionnaire answers are most important for predicting voting patterns, and how the model went wrong: visualise the distribution of what the model predicted versus true voting intentions
  3. Model predictions — interactively explore how altering non-demographic values/beliefs might change voting intentions
  4. Examine voting intention breakdown along features that were important for predicting voting patterns
  5. Explore economic/social values of persuadable/uncommitted voters

1 · Values Exploration

Where voters sit on economic and nationalistic values

Each dot is one person, placed by their answers to 10 attitude questions. Left–right is the nationalism–internationalism scale (internationalist/open on the left, nationalist/closed on the right); bottom–top is the economic scale (economic left at the bottom, economic right at the top). Colours show who they'd vote for. Click a party in the legend to show or hide it.

Labour and Lib Dem voters cluster in the bottom-left — economically left and internationalist/open. Conservatives sit higher up (economic right) and spread across the nationalism axis. The diamonds show each party's average position; error bars span the 25th–75th percentile (IQR).

Which answers matter most?

Two ways to read importance. Importance for model: how much accuracy drops if you scramble just that group of answers (what it adds on top of everything else). Standalone power: how well that group predicts on its own. Stars mark statistical significance (one-sample t-test over cross-validation folds). Error bars show the 95% confidence interval of the mean across the cross-validation folds.

How does the model get things wrong?

Each row is the people who actually intend to vote for that party; the colours show what the model predicted for them. A strong diagonal means accurate predictions. Read along a row to see who gets confused with whom.

5 · Persuadable voters

Who could be won over?

The model predicts, from each respondent's survey answers, how likely they are to vote for each party. Pick a target party to find the people who look most like its voters—based on their demographics, values, and background—but aren't yet committed to it. Uncommitted shows undecided or won't-vote respondents who, if they were to commit, the model thinks would most likely choose your target party. Committed elsewhere shows voters currently supporting another party but whom the model thinks fit your target party's profile nearly as well - either it predicted your party first (got it wrong), or your party was their second-best match.

Start by picking a target party and choosing which group to analyse (uncommitted voters or those committed to other parties). The Profile by dropdown lets you break down the group by demographics. The How many people slider shows the top N most persuadable candidates, ranked by how likely the model thinks they are to vote for your target party—the left chart shows their average values on the economic and nationlistic axes vs committed voters, and the right chart breaks down their demographics compared to the general population.

Persuadable individuals' average values vs the party's committed voters

The circle represents the average position of voters already committed to the target party. The star shows the average position of the persuadable group you selected above. If the star is close to the circle, persuadable voters align well with the party's existing base on values. The distance shows how much work it might take to move voters over, or how naturally aligned they already are.

Their make-up vs everyone else

The left bar shows the demographic make-up of the persuadable group (age, region, work sector, etc.). The right bar is the population average, for comparison.
